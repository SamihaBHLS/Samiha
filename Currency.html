Currency Exchange Tips

Best Places to Exchange

  • Local banks often offer better rates than airports
  • Use ATMs for good rates (check your bank's fees)
  • Avoid hotel currency exchanges - they have high fees
  • Compare rates at multiple exchange offices

Using Cards Abroad

  • Notify your bank before traveling internationally
  • Use credit cards with no foreign transaction fees
  • Always pay in local currency to avoid bad rates
  • Carry a backup card in case one gets blocked

Cash Management

  • Carry small denominations for tips and taxis
  • Don't exchange all your money at once
  • Keep emergency cash separate from your wallet
  • Know the local customs for tipping

Currency Conversion FAQs

The best exchange rates are typically found at local banks, credit unions, or reputable currency exchange offices in major cities. Avoid exchanging money at airports, hotels, or tourist areas as they usually offer poor rates with high fees. Using ATMs in your destination country often provides competitive rates, but check with your bank about foreign transaction fees first.

It's good to exchange a small amount of money before you travel (enough for transportation and immediate expenses), but don't exchange all your money at home. You'll generally get better rates in the country you're visiting. However, for some currencies that are difficult to exchange abroad or countries with strict currency controls, you may need to get more in advance.

It depends on your destination. In most developed countries, credit cards (especially those without foreign transaction fees) are convenient and offer good exchange rates. However, always carry some local cash as smaller establishments may not accept cards. In some countries, cash is still king, so research your destination's payment preferences before you go.

Exchange rates fluctuate constantly throughout the day as currencies are traded on global markets. Major economic news, political events, and market sentiment can cause significant movements. For travelers, the rates at banks and exchange offices may only update once or twice a day, while credit card companies typically use the daily interbank rate at the time of transaction.

Options for leftover currency include: exchanging it back to your home currency (though you'll lose on the spread), keeping it for a future trip, donating it to charity (some airports have donation boxes), or giving it to friends who might be traveling to that country. For coins, consider spending them at the airport before departure as many places won't exchange coins.
