Vaccine Requirements by Region

African Vaccination Requirements

Sub-Saharan Africa High Risk

Many countries require yellow fever vaccination certificate.

  • Required: Yellow fever for many countries
  • Recommended: Typhoid, Hepatitis A & B
  • Malaria: Prophylaxis recommended in most areas
  • Cholera: Risk in some regions
  • Meningitis: Belt region during dry season

North Africa Medium Risk

Generally lower risk than sub-Saharan Africa but still important precautions.

  • Required: None typically
  • Recommended: Hepatitis A, Typhoid
  • Malaria: Limited risk in some areas
  • COVID-19: Check current entry requirements
  • Water Safety: Bottled water recommended

Special Considerations

Additional health precautions for African travel.

  • Proof: Carry yellow fever certificate
  • Rabies: Consider if outdoor activities
  • Waterborne: Drink only bottled/purified water
  • HIV: Universal precautions recommended
  • Ebola: Check for current outbreaks

Asian Vaccination Requirements

Southeast Asia Medium Risk

Tropical diseases more common in rural areas.

  • Required: Yellow fever if coming from endemic area
  • Recommended: Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid, Japanese Encephalitis
  • Malaria: Risk in rural areas
  • Dengue: Mosquito protection essential
  • Food Safety: Street food precautions

South Asia High Risk

Higher risk of food/waterborne illnesses.

  • Required: Polio if coming from affected countries
  • Recommended: All routine plus Hepatitis A, Typhoid
  • Malaria: High risk in many areas
  • Cholera: Sporadic outbreaks
  • Rabies: Consider pre-exposure vaccine

East Asia Lower Risk

Generally good health infrastructure.

  • Required: None typically
  • Recommended: Routine vaccines, Hepatitis A
  • Special: Japanese Encephalitis for rural areas
  • COVID-19: Check current restrictions
  • Flu Season: Consider influenza vaccine

Vaccination Preparation Timeline

1

Research (6-8 weeks before)

Check CDC/WHO recommendations for your destination. Identify required and recommended vaccines.

2

Medical Consultation (4-6 weeks before)

Visit a travel medicine specialist to review your immunization history and get professional advice.

3

Vaccination Schedule (3-6 weeks before)

Some vaccines require multiple doses over several weeks. Others need time to become effective.

4

Documentation (1-2 weeks before)

Obtain International Certificate of Vaccination (yellow card) for required vaccines like yellow fever.

5

Final Check (1 week before)

Verify all vaccinations are complete. Pack your vaccination records with your travel documents.

6

During Travel

Continue malaria prophylaxis if prescribed. Practice food/water safety and mosquito bite prevention.

Common Travel Vaccines

Yellow Fever

  • Required for: Many African and South American countries
  • Duration: Lifetime for most travelers
  • Side Effects: Mild fever, headache common
  • Certification: International Certificate required

Typhoid

  • Recommended for: Most developing countries
  • Types: Injectable (2 years) or oral (5 years)
  • Protection: 50-80% effective
  • Also: Still practice food/water precautions

Hepatitis A

  • Recommended for: All international travel
  • Schedule: 2 doses (6-12 months apart)
  • Duration: Likely lifetime protection
  • Combination: Available with Hepatitis B

Japanese Encephalitis

  • Recommended for: Rural Asia, prolonged stays
  • Schedule: 2 doses (28 days apart)
  • Boosters: May be needed after 1-2 years
  • Risk: Low for most travelers

Cholera

  • Recommended for: Humanitarian workers in outbreaks
  • Type: Oral vaccine (2-3 doses)
  • Protection: 65% effective for 6 months
  • Also: Still essential to drink safe water

Rabies

  • Recommended for: Long stays, remote areas
  • Schedule: 3 doses over 3-4 weeks
  • Post-exposure: Still needs treatment
  • Benefit: Buys time to reach care

Vaccination FAQs

Ideally, schedule a travel health consultation 4-6 weeks before departure. Many vaccines require multiple doses over several weeks to provide full protection. Some vaccines (like rabies) require a month to complete the series. Even last-minute travelers should get vaccinated as some protection is better than none.

Coverage varies by insurance provider. Routine vaccines (like MMR or flu) are often covered, but travel-specific vaccines (yellow fever, typhoid) may not be. Some plans cover vaccines if administered by your primary doctor but not at travel clinics. Always check with your insurer beforehand.

Even partial vaccination provides some protection. Some vaccines can be accelerated (like hepatitis B). For diseases like rabies, starting the series before travel means you'd need fewer post-exposure shots. Focus first on required vaccines (yellow fever) and highest risk diseases for your destination.

Yes, most travel vaccines can be given simultaneously at different injection sites. Exceptions include live vaccines (like yellow fever and MMR) which should either be given on the same day or spaced 4 weeks apart. Your travel health provider will create an optimal schedule.

Most side effects are mild: soreness at injection site, low-grade fever, headache, or fatigue. Yellow fever vaccine may cause flu-like symptoms 5-10 days after vaccination. Serious allergic reactions are rare. Always stay at the clinic for 15 minutes after vaccination as a precaution.
