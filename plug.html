Travel Power Tips

Adapter vs Converter

  • Adapter: Only changes plug shape, not voltage
  • Converter: Changes voltage for devices not dual-voltage
  • Check device labels for "Input: 100-240V" - these are dual-voltage
  • Hair dryers, curling irons often need converters

Device Charging

  • Most modern electronics (laptops, phones) are dual-voltage
  • USB chargers typically work worldwide (check label)
  • Bring a multi-port USB charger to reduce adapters needed
  • Consider a portable power bank for emergencies

Packing Essentials

  • Universal adapter with multiple plug types
  • Compact power strip for multiple devices
  • Voltage converter if needed for specific devices
  • Extra USB cables and chargers

Plug Adapter FAQs

It depends on your devices. Most modern electronics like laptops, phones, and cameras are dual-voltage (check for "Input: 100-240V" on the charger). These only need a plug adapter. Devices with heating elements (hair dryers, curling irons) or motors often require a voltage converter. Using a device with the wrong voltage can damage it or be dangerous.

The best universal adapters cover all major plug types (A/B, C, E/F, G, I) and include USB ports. Look for features like: compact design, surge protection, ability to handle high wattage (for hair dryers), and multiple USB ports. Some top-rated brands include Ceptics, BESTEK, and OREI. Avoid the cheapest options as they may not be safety certified.

However, devices designed only for 110-120V (like some hair tools, kitchen appliances) may overheat or fail on Europe's 220-240V system. Always check the voltage rating on each device before plugging it in.

Many international hotels install special 110V outlets in bathrooms (often marked "for shavers only") to accommodate travelers from North America and Japan. These are typically limited to low-wattage devices. Some higher-end hotels may also provide universal outlets at the desk. Always check with the front desk about outlet types before plugging in valuable electronics.

First, unplug the device immediately. Check if there's a reset button or fuse in the plug. For dual-voltage devices, ensure the voltage switch (if present) is set correctly. The device may have an internal fuse that blew - consult the manual. If the device was not dual-voltage, it may be permanently damaged. In some countries, you can find replacement fuses at electronics stores.
